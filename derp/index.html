Crowdjudged relationships: Research Proposal for DERP Institute

Motivation

The world is becoming divided and it is imperative to determine the nature relationship between people on social networks. The users leave evidence of their relationships with others as they interact publicly on the networks via comments and voting. However, understanding the comments and the intent of the users behind them is a difficult task; tools such as sentiment analysis often too crude and miss too much context to be able to help us effectively.

In this project, we will explore ways we can leverage (voting) actions taken by other members of the crowd to uncover the relationship between users who interact often on the site. Discovering such an (dis)agreement network among the users of the site may lead to several interesting and actionable insights:

  • Users who always agree across nearly all topics may be astroturfing or be bots.
  • Users who almost always disagree with everyone else may be trolls.
  • Targeting articles to users depending on who has commented on it (i.e., sorting by (dis)agreement among currently active commenters relative to the recommended).

It will also be interesting to study the evolution of relationships between users in an online incremental manner by discounting older assessments and incorporating newer ones. This will allow us to determine the key articles which were useful in reconciling commenters. We will also be able to identify orchestrated attacks which will appear as temporally localize strong agreement between commenters.

This person can then choose to cast votes on the parent comment and the reply. These votes can tell us the relative positioning of the opinions expressed in the comments with respect to the voter's comments.

If sufficient number of votes cast such votes, we ought to be able to uncover the relative degree of (dis)agreement between the comments. Aggregating the difference between comments cast on same-topic articles by the same commenters can lead to determination of degree of (dis)agreement between commenters on the topic.

Data available & data required

The comments cast by users on the site are public. However, the public information is missing key pieces of information which are crucial for our project:

  1. Identity of voters/commenters: The public data does not identify the voters which are casting the up/down vote on the comments. Only if we know that the same voter cast two votes will we be able to relatively position the opinions expressed in the comments.

  2. Timestamps for votes/comments: Knowing the timestamps will helps us in discovering the evolution of votes a post was getting and the evolution of opinions of the voters.

  3. Metadata about the comments: The URL of the article, the body of the comment, the Avatar of the user and any data on flagging/moderation will be useful to have.

The study will be made more interesting if we have datasets about comments from multiple diverse communities to see the similarities and differences in the underlying networks among them.
